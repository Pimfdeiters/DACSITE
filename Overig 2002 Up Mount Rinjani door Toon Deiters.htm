Up Mount Rinjani, twice (3750M)

 

1. Ora et Labora, 1990

 

Some of you have heard this story. I told it at the second night dinner of the DAC trip in 1999. If you weren’t overcome by the effects of  either that famous twelve-hour hike or the free-flowing wine, you may even remember it. I believe it deserves a place in the DAC records, so any member may have access to it.

 

I was on Lombok, one of the lesser Sunda Islands of Indonesia, September 7 through 10, 1990 with a  group of fifteen hikers and climbers known as the Java Lavas. Volcano climbing was our raison d’etre, and Indonesia has more of these conical protrusions than there are members of the Deiters clan. This time we had our sights on Gunung (Mount) Rinjani, the second highest volcano in the archipelago. (The highest one is Gunung Kerinci, on Sumatra; we had climbed that one in October 1989.) Rinjani forms the entire northern half of Lombok, a huge massif with a crater several kilometers in diameter.  The point of departure, a dessa (village) on the northern slopes a few kilometers from the sea, was reached in a three hour bus ride from Mataram. The bus was not air-conditioned, the seats were barely covered, the roads rather bumpy, but what’s wrong  with a little hardship after the luxuries of Jakarta?

 

At the staging area we spread out the supplies for the expedition, which would involve two overnight camps: one on the way up, and the other, at the edge of the crater lake, on the way down. Porters had been arranged but were late. The afternoon dragged on to near dusk, when at last they showed up. Their main task was to carry water and food, and heavy camping equipment. Nearby were fields with crops of onions and carrots, and women tending them. I talked to the driver of a small pick-up truck who was waiting nearby to haul the women back to their village. He was an older man, his face testimony to a life of toil, and he spoke some dutch.  His final words, however, were latin. As he sat behind the wheel and revved up the old engine, he said, to my utter surprise: “Ora et labora.” Lombok is a heavily-muslim island. Had he been an altar boy in the catholic church? He drove away with his human load before I could clear this up. I had no clue of the significance, let alone  prophetic power, of his words. All I felt was that this would be no ordinary climb.

 

The climb proved to be a major test for me. I had boldly elected to join an eager group of  five of our  younger members, all British, that wanted to skip the first camp and go straight through the night (when it is not just cool, but cold!) for the summit, a vertical climb of 2700 meters. This is far more than one ever does in the Alps in one stretch. Add to that a huge horizontal distance, and you have a severe endurance test, and, in my case, the recipe for failure. The other nine people of our group were more sensible and camped after dinner near a spring located by the last fields, close to the edge of the jungle. Only one of them would make it  to the summit.

 

Our small group, led by a tough Scotsman who was rumored to be a former commando, pushed through the moonlit night in punishing speed. The trail was in good shape, at times a bit steep but manageable. It seemed endless. At 4 AM, less than two hundred meters below the crater’s edge, at an altitude of  2900 meters, a tall, hunched, BP-geologist nicknamed Turtle and I decided to take a break. We rolled out our sleeping bags, and lodged ourselves against some shrubs so we would not slide downhill. Turtle slept, he said later; I did not. Although I had changed into dry underwear, I was cold and uncomfortable. I lay waiting for sunrise, which came, mercifully, at around six. Shivering, Turtle and I broke camp and set off to the crater’s edge above us, to the beginning of the summit ridge. On smaller volcanoes, reaching the edge of the crater is often tantamount to making the summit. Not so on Rinjani. The circumference of the entire crater amounts to over twenty kilometers. From the spot where our trail hit the rim, it would be a 700M vertical climb, over a distance of about three kilometers, to the summit. (Compare this to the 800M vertical climb from the Dent Blanche hut at 3500M to the summit at 4300M.) The terrain was dangerous. While the progression along the rim to the summit  was not overly steep, just like the Biancograt of Piz Bernina, a fall to either side would be fatal. On the north side, to our left, there was the steep scree field that characterizes the summit cone of most volcanoes. Lots of loose stones, and no ridges to stop you. If you fall and gather speed it is almost impossible to arrest yourself.  On the south side was the gaping abyss, a drop of about a kilometer at the spot where we joined the ridge and of 1700M at the summit,  to the bottom of the crater. The sun, the magnificent view and the effort soon made me forget the chills of the night. It was a brilliant day, and Turtle and I were making slow but steady progress. 

 

We had been going for a couple of hours when I needed badly to  take a rest. Although I didn’t feel thirsty,  I forced myself to drink. I knew of the dangers of dehydration and exhaustion, and vaguely realized I might not have been drinking enough or replaced the salt I had lost sweating. I sat down in the lea of a huge boulder, while Turtle continued. To the west I could see Gunung Agung on Bali, and to the east Mt. Tambora on Sumbawa. Then I became overwhelmed by fatigue and fell into an uneasy sleep.

 

I was awakened by two friends on their way down from the summit. They told me I was about 200M vertical climb below the summit, at a distance of  less than a kilometer, and encouraged me to give it a try. They went on down, direction crater lake, where camp two would be ready. I tried to get up but my legs were not cooperating. I slept some more. After some time a group of Indonesian pilgrims came struggling by, some wearing sandals. Three men pulling (by rolled up kain, or cloth) and pushing (hands to rear-end) an older woman. We had passed this group on our way up, it was their third day on the mountain. Step by slow step they worked their way up to pray for good crops, health and fortune. Half an hour later I was pleased to see the first, and only, member of  the group that had camped below.  It was my friend Ilham. A Turk with a British passport, Ilham is short, gentle and polite to a fault. He is also tough and determined. He and I discussed the merits of my going up with him. Overcoming my lethargy, I struggled for perhaps a  hundred meters but in vain. I felt that the summit was not in the cards. My weakened will said yes, my body just said no. I had the strange sensation that forces bigger than I were deciding things for me. I returned to my rock.

 

Eventually I got back on my feet, but it was too late to go for the summit. I went down with the next two summiters of the front group, with Ilham and Turtle following behind. We joined the rest of the pack near the spot where Turtle and I had come to the rim. From there we had a spectacular view of the crater whose bottom was largely covered by a bluish lake, with steeply below us, one kilometer down, the small beach where we were to camp that night. It was still a difficult descent over a steep and sometimes slippery trail. The swim, food, beer and comfort at the lower altitude, all combined to make it an enjoyable camp. I forgave myself for not having made the summit: only twice before did I have to forego a summit while on my way, the Nadelhorn in 1962 and the Monte Rosa in 1963; both because of bad weather. Such excuse did not apply this time. I quietly made up my mind to come back one day and finish the job.

 

Back in Jakarta a few days later, I had a call from Ann who was in Washington then. She asked: “have you heard that Oom Ben died?”  I hadn’t, and decided to call Els to offer my condolences.  In our conversation I recounted my strange experience on the mountain, my complete exhaustion. We calculated that Oom Ben had died  around the time of my reaching camp 2.

 

A few weeks later we received in the mail the death-announcement, featuring Oom Ben’s portrait on the outside. When I looked within, to read the actual announcement, it jumped right at me. His motto, “Ora et Labora”. The very same, last words of the old truck driver. I resolved that not only  would I go back to Rinjani, I would climb it in honor of, in memoriam,  Oom Ben.

 

2. To the summit

in memoriam Oom Ben, 1994

 

I got my chance in the month of August, 1994. In April of that year, Ann and I had been hiking up Gunung Gede, that magnificent volcano in the Puncak, between Jakarta, Sukabumi and Bandung. We were with a group of young Dutch enthusiasts, who had been brought together by Marc, the field captain of our hockey team, Kombinasi. Marc had served with the Dutch Marines, and loved exploring his new home, Indonesia. He had done rafting, scubadiving and other exciting things but he had never been up a mountain. His only experience with hilly terrain had been the knolls he had to crawl up as a Marine. He asked me to help him organize a weekend of hiking and rafting. He would arrange the rafting part while I would lead the climb. I gladly agreed.

 

The traverse of Mt Gede (2970M), climbing up from the Puncak on the north-east side and descending into Sukabumi on the south slope, was a first volcano climb for most of the participants. The summit was reached in bright sunlight, but the descent  had to be done in a torrential downpour, which turned the trails into small rivers. We were soaked, muddy and full of leeches by the time we got to the first dessa near Sukabumi,  fourteen hours after we had departed from the Puncak. We spent the night in luxury at Pelabuan Ratu, on the Indian Ocean, gorging on grilled lobster and beer, and resting up for the next leg of this expedition, a white-water rafting trip down one of the fast flowing rivers through the jungle to the coastal plain around Pelabuan Ratu.

 

The weekend was, by all accounts, a great success. There were calls for a repeat, but this time something really big, involving camping. I proposed Rinjani. That is why, a few months later, Ann and I found ourselves on Senggigi beach, outside Mataram on the west coast of Lombok, staring at the fading contours of Gunung Agung on Bali. We had a quiet evening: the other participants, five young Dutch couples averaging 32 years in age would arrive the next day.

 

That evening I heard some alarming rumors. Six weeks earlier the people in Mataram had been hearing the sound of distant artillery, loud explosive sounds. It appeared that  Mt Rinjani had erupted in a major way, and had been declared off limits. Huge plumes of  spewed ash had been visible, and we heard that a layer of heavy, poisonous gas was hanging over the crater lake with nowhere to go.  I was also told that the mountain had been quiet for more than a month now. An important decision lay ahead. I wanted to discuss the situation with Mark but had already made up my mind: we would go ahead anyway, and see how far we could go.

 

The group arrived the next day. We frolicked near the pool, rested, bought last minute supplies, checked our gear. I arranged transportation for departure the following morning. What we didn’t need we could leave in storage at the hotel. At dinner -- nasi goreng and beer -- I brought up the matter of the recent eruptions. Nobody had qualms. We all agreed to go ahead.

 

The bus trip to the dessa  was uneventful. But once there, it would be a few hours before we could get going. From the Kepala Dessa (Village Chief) I hired twenty able-bodied men as porters, but they were not all immediately available. Then I had to meet with the Chief of Police. He told me that he would give us a permit to go up to a flat shoulder a bit below the point where Turtle and I had spent a few hours resting, but absolutely no further. And in no event were we to descend into the crater to the lake. I promised. And soon, with well over three hours of daylight ahead of us, we were on our way.

 

My plan was simple. We would only succeed if we took our time. So three camps were planned. Our first camp, roughly at the same place where nine of our group had camped four years before, would be reached before dusk. Tents would be pitched,  a fire made and plenty of water be available from a nearby spring. Meals were quickly cooked by adding water to dried supermie or nasi-goreng. We all slept very well. The next day gave me a chance to actually see what I had covered in the dark in 1990. A beautiful forest, interrupted by huge stands of waving tall grass. The trail was only steep in parts, but well curved and making excellent use of the mountain’s contours. We made frequent resting stops, and waited for those who were going at a slower pace. Nobody had trouble of any kind. The weather was good, a bit breezy perhaps, but pleasant enough.  After doing a series of slow hairpins, we came to a heavily grown ridge, which kept rising mildly and led to the promontory where we would make camp. It was tea time, the sun to the west. Through a breach in the crater wall that we had used four years ago for our descent to the lake, we could see part of the  crater . It seemed a lot less green than I remembered and the lake seemed different in shape.  We speculated about the gas hanging down there, and had tea.

 

As it turned out, we were not alone. A family of monkeys was calling this place home, and they were not shy. We had bought up fresh vegetables from the farmers below, and these were going to be part of a delicious stew. The monkeys thought differently. We put the bags on a ledge in front of the tents, and went about our preparations for the night and the next morning, while daylight lasted. There was a scream from Karin, who would be making the stew. The bag was gone, and about 20M below two monkeys were fighting over a carrot. We would be having packaged food again that night.

 

Before dinner Ann led a yoga session as she had done the day before. Most of us participated, using our camping mattrasses as a base.  Dinner tasted good, we felt fit and at ease at the 2800M altitude. The next morning we would go up to the rim, and then I would break my promise to the police, taking with me whomever wanted to join me in going to the summit, to fulfill the more important promise I had made four years earlier.

 

It had been breezy throughout the day, but overnight the wind had picked up. When we rose at day break, eight of us set out along the trail to the ridge. I recognized certain passages where there were fixed ropes and ladders on a steep stretch. Then suddenly, we were at the rim, and had a first real peek into the crater right below us. The sight was astonishing. What had been green -- the stands of pine, the shrubs, the tall grass that covered the gentler inside slopes of the enormous crater -- was all gone; covered by a blanket of  black-gray volcanic material. A sight of death and devastation. A new cone had formed where the eruptions had occurred, a small mountain within the mountain: “Anak Gunung Rinjani”, as one of the porters called it, “Child of Rinjani.”

 

As we carefully made our trail up along the ridge toward the summit, the view soon became blurred. The whole ridge was covered with at least ten centimeters of fine black ash, that made us feel like we were walking on a soft carpet. The ash, however, was so light and fine that it was blown by the now fierce wind into ominous clouds. It became hard to breathe, and like cowboys, we covered our nose and mouth with a handkerchief. The wind became stronger still, and was howling. At least it came from the north, the sea, so I figured there was no danger of it carrying poisonous gas. But that meant it blew into the direction of the crater’s abyss. We had a brief discussion, and five of our group decided to return. Marc and Ronald went on with me, at their own risk. After a while Marc decided it was his time to turn around. I went on alone, there was no sight of Ronald. The wind became so strong that I had to crawl up on all fours, lest I be swept off the ridge. I had visions of my climb of the Bernina over the Biancograt in 1987, when my guide shouted to me through the howling storm: “es ist jetzt ausserordentlich gefaehrlich,” and short-roped me up that knife-edged ridge, both of us crawling like tigers. But my resolve was clear: I was going for the summit even if I’d have to crawl all day.

 

I passed the spot with the boulder where I had collapsed fours year before, and I knew that now I was within striking range. There was no turning back. I crawled on over the ash-covered ridge, talking to myself to drown my fear of being blown off, and to urge myself on. I must have crawled for hours when I came to a strange rock formation blocking my path. There was no more ridge behind it, so it seemed. Was this the summit? A look at my altitude meter confirmed that I had crossed the 3700M level. I sat down in a hollow formed by the rock that sheltered me from the wind. Then, to my great relief, the wind abated, and the swirls of black cloud were only below me: I appeared to be above the storm, like a plane flying above the clouds. I scanned my surroundings, and found that the ridge which had been going east now bent to the south. It descended, but was it rising after the first dip? I had heard that there was a cairn marking the summit. I decided to explore the ridge south. It proved to be the right decision. Twenty minutes later I stood on top of Mount Rinjani. The wind was light, the sky bright, the view magnificent. I took my telescopic pole out of my pack. It had been of little use during my long crawl, but now I needed it as identification for my summit picture. I planted the pole into the stones of the cairn, and then wrote the words “Oom Ben” in the smooth bed of ash in front of it. The photo I took came out well. One day I will scan it into this story-text.

 

Before I turned around to go back to my shelter at the rock for some food and drink, I noticed  something sticking out of the ash on the other side of the cairn. Something skin-like. I probed with my pole and to my astonishment I dug up the mummified remains of a monkey; I took a picture of it. It must have had its own reasons for pursuing the ridge to its apex, which turned out to be a point of no return. How long had it been there? I felt some sympathy for its surviving relatives who were probably still feasting on our vegetables.

 

As I enjoyed my brunch and the landscape from my high perch, two tiny figures appeared out of the still dark swirls below. Twenty minutes later Ronald, and a German named Richard whom we had encountered the previous day, were standing in front of me. We all went to the summit for a new photo session, this time resulting in shots of live bodies.

 

The descent was long and tricky at times. But the winds were not as strong and we could walk upright. The reunion at the campsite was lively. The photo taken of Ann and me embracing still hangs in our Ridgefield kitchen. My face is black.

 

We enjoyed another night on the mountain, in a lower camp, did yoga and ate and slept well. I bribed the boss of our porters to keep our summit drive secret. It must have worked for we reached Mataram without incident. We spent the last night together at Senggigi beach, with showers, pool and sea to refresh us, and a feast of delicacies, beer and wine to celebrate our success and safe return.

 

A few days later, back in Jakarta, I called Ilham to tell him that this time I had made it to the summit. Ilham and I had done quite a few climbs together in the intermittent four years. Our first climb had been in October 1989, to the highest volcano of all, Gunung Kerinci, in central west Sumatra. That had been my very first trip with the Java Lavas, and a tall Dutchman named Ben Blijleven had led it. Ben enjoyed great respect and popularity among the members of the Java Lavas, as well as in that circle of men who enjoy a beer after a run in the jungle, the Hash House Harriers. He owed his nickname, Oom Ben, to the Jakarta Hash.

 

I had barely uttered a word on the phone when Ilham interrupted me. “Did you hear Oom Ben died?” It hit me like a bolt of lightning. Was this one of those famous deja-vu experiences? Was this real? But there it was. During the long weekend of our Rinjani climb, our friend, fellow climber and hasher (Oom) Ben Blijleven had died of a heart attack after a squash game in Rotterdam. He had been a helicopter pilot for Schreiner and had worked in many countries. He was repatriated from Indonesia in 1992. Of all places he had been to, he loved that country most. His ashes were later to be scattered over Gunung Gede and the Java Sea.

 

There are people, among them the famous Swiss philosopher/psychiatrist Carl Gustav Jung, who say that a coincidence often is more than meets the eye. They speak of synchronicity, or meaningful coincidence. Looking back after so many years on these events, and remembering how much Oom Ben Deiters, like my own father, encouraged my first steps in mountaineering; and how much Oom Ben Blijleven loved hiking the volcanoes of Indonesia, I can only say that Jung makes more sense to me now. And may both Ooms Ben rest in peace.

 

Anton B. Deiters

Ridgefield, CT, July 2002
