The Day after the Bernina             August 1987                    Anton Deiters

 

(Translated from the Dutch and revised in 2009; original title: “Daags na de Bernina,” written in January 1988, based on notes taken in August 1987)

 

“N’oublie jamais que ca commence ici” (A guide from Chamonix, 1963)

 

It is pleasantly warm. Swarms of flies are buzzing around the still smoking dung heaps dropped by carriage horses in Val Fex. Butterflies and walkers are abundant. The grass has been mown and much of the hay has been collected. Farmsteads and vacation chalets are sitting peacefully together, the farming element being more in evidence. This valley has not (yet) been developed.

 

You encounter no joggers here, no mountain-bikers; no determined faces with wet sweatbands over foreheads. Val Fex is bucolic. The mountains are merely backdrop; the stage itself is green, agrarian, lush. Humming a tune, I walk to the small inn where Ann and some of my sisters are working on a sun tan. Save for a mild soreness of the thighs, I feel great.

 

 

Yesterday, I was walking in another valley, descending from the Boval Hut, after having climbed Piz Bernina via the Bianco Grat. There had been a fright at that hut. Ann and Patricia (my sister # 6), who had hiked up to it to welcome me back, had been scared when they heard that there had been an accident in “das Buch,” a potential dangerous section of the north-east face of the Bernina. Apparently, some rope-parties had made a short-cut. People had fallen into a crevasse. The guardian of the Boval Hut, using his powerful telescope, had seen someone in distress, waving something bright red. He had become quite excited, for, as was I, his own son was expected at the Boval Hut after having summited the Bernina via Bianco Grat. He had immediately signaled for help. A helicopter from Samedan airport was on the scene within fifteen minutes. And some time later, near the landing platform of the hut where the helicopter made a first-aid stop, Ann and Patricia found out to their great relief that I was not one the two seriously injured, rescued climbers.

 

I had seen this happen as well, from the Fortezza Grat, that long, in-part rocky, ridge which is also the way down of the Piz Palu’s “three summit traverse.” We had heard a whistled distress signal. At “eight o’clock” (behind our left shoulder) about two kilometers away and at an elevation of a few hundred yards below us, my guide – a man from Berguenn called Hans, a top-notch climber who soloed Eiger’s North Face -- and I saw, through my binoculars, someone waving frantically. Hans who had already noted three black dots in that dangerous terrain said he was not surprised that something had gone wrong. The night before, in the Tschierva Hut, there had been a lively discussion about the shorter route through “Das Buch.”  Hans and a few others had strongly advised against using that route, on account of the many crevasses and the unreliability of the snow bridges due to warm weather. What made the short-cut attractive, however, was a two-hour earlier arrival at the Boval Hut. Hans and I had decided to use the safer and longer descent route, via the “Fortezza Grat.”

 

We had had a fast start from the Tschierva Hut, that early, pre-dawn hour and were the first of six rope-parties to exit the steep ice wall at the top part of the Tschierva Glacier at Fuorcla Prievlusa, the saddle between the Morteratsch and the Bernina and reach the ridge that would lead us to the summit, the famous, knife-edged Bianco Grat. The first section turned out to consist of large rock formations. The weather looked still pretty good, although the guide was a bit puzzled by “a remarkably strong wind” which greeted us as soon as we had left the lee-side of the mountain. That wind became stronger by the minute, turning the guide’s puzzlement into serious concern. When we reached the snow ridge, the real Bianco Grat, a full gale-force storm was blowing. The guide warned me, ”es ist jezt ausserordentlich gefaerlich” (it is now extraordinarily dangerous), a warning I had never heard put so explicitly.

 

 

He short-roped me, and like tigers, our bodies pressed to the snow, we crept up on all fours to give the storm as small a target as possible. “If I am blown to the right, you jump to the left,” he had said when we started. Due to the roar of the storm, communication was virtually impossible. After the long snow ridge followed the rock ridge to the summit. Here we had to proceed on a fully uncoiled rope, belaying each other. This final, one hour rock-climb to the summit was even more frightening. There was snow and ice on the rocks and the drops on all sides were almost vertical. The margin for error was zero. Once in a while, the heavy rope stood like a rainbow in the sky, propelled by the storm. We reached the summit a little over six hours after our departure from the Tschierva Hut and followed the ridge toward Piz Csercsen for some hundred yards; then began our descent, rappelling to the smooth floor of the relatively wind-free  Swiss-Italian border snow field. Our goal was the Boval Hut, where my 15 year old son Erik was spending the week, taking part in a climbing course led by Pontresina mountain guides, and where I would be awaited by Ann and Patricia.

 

Hans is in a hurry. He wants to reach the Diavolezza Hut because there is money to be made there. As we descend the Fortezza Grat, he proposes that we skip the Boval Hut and, instead, cross the Diavolezza Glacier and ascend its far bank to the large Diavolezza Hut. He estimates that he won’t have enough time to first hit the Boval Hut, then descend to the valley and catch the last cable car to Diavolezza. I remind him of our arrangement:  “Meet me at the Tschierva, deliver me at the Boval.” But then, all of a sudden, this “commercial” disagreement becomes entirely academic, because we see someone waving a red object, apparently in distress. I silently congratulate us with last night’s decision to take the longer route. But now it is our duty to come to the rescue.

 

 

As we prepare to make a quick descent to the Morteratsch Glacier by a series of rappels in order to climb up to “Das Buch” and reach the crevasse into which one or two persons have disappeared, connected by rope to their guide, who is frantically waving a red sweater, we hear the sound of a helicopter, approaching fast. It passes a little below us, following the midline of the Morteratsch glacier, heading straight for the scene of the accident. We are off the hook, immensely relieved. Without further argument, Hans takes me to the Boval Hut. We descend the full extent of the Fortezza Grat, negotiate Isola Persa and cross the Morteratsch Glacier a little downstream from it.

 

It is at least a two hour hike from the point where the helicopter passed us till we arrive at the Boval Hut at ten past four, well over twelve hours after we had left the Tschierva Hut. I am welcomed warmly by Ann and Patricia, who are very happy to see me alive and well. Together with the guardian, we reconstruct the events of the day. Half an hour later, Erik appears at the hut after his rock climbing exercises. He tells us that, early that morning, his group had been on its way to climb the Piz Palu; they had to cancel that climb due to “a raging storm that would have blown us off the Palu summit ridge into Italy.”

 

I spend a few more hours with Erik; the girls have already left for the valley. I tell him about the 50s, when I came here with my parents in ’52; camped with school friends in ’59, the year when my parents had been very worried about me. They were in Italy, in the Dolomites – where I was to join them soon after -- and read in a Dutch newspaper that two Dutchmen had fatally fallen in the Bernina area. They turned out to be the promising Michielsen brothers whom I had met in ‘58 at a meeting of the Royal Netherlands Alpine Club, where they had given a fascinating presentation. I explain to Erik that there are areas to which one simply must return, to find closure. Later, as I descend alone to the valley, to Pontresina, I notice how much the Morteratsch Glacier has shrunk. Had my father not observed the same, when we children, Guus, Monique, Helene, Pia and I, with our funny little back-packs, hiked with our parents to the Boval Hut in 1952?

 

 

Now it’s Val Fex, the day after the Bianco Grat. I feel light. Patches of mountain memories are being stitched together into a colorful quilt. In the past, I kept diaries of mountain vacations. Now I feel as if I could compress all my experiences -- all those vacations, the hiking, pack-carrying, scrambling, and climbing; all that snow, sun, rock and ice – into one paragraph, or a poem perhaps. The wind gusts of yesterday are those of November ’76 in Nepal, when I was part of a small Dutch expedition. There, in Camp 2 on the Phrul-ri just after midnight, the storm broke the frontal guy line of our small tent, exposing Willem Jan and me to a beautiful but ice-cold starry firmament. Making the best of a bad situation, we built a sort of igloo around the tent, and cut a few feet off our least important rope to re-anchor the tent. Days later, during the meditative six-day hike back to the world of Kathmandu, we walked one morning in the company of a monk wearing dark-red robes. He was not the Dalai Lama, but could have easily passed for him, with his glasses, sandals and ready grin. This monk offered us fresh water from a spring whose location he knew, saying “do not forget this spot where we drank together.”

 

I carry this memory somewhere inside me. It has become a bit blurred, in spite of – or, perhaps, because of – the many slide shows I have given after the Himalaya trip. But here, in Val Fex, it comes back to me in graphic and colorful detail. Climbing always implies hiking through valleys. Aristotle asked: “what is more important, action or contemplation?” His own answer: “contemplation.” I sometimes pose that same question myself; my answer, however, is less categorical. To me, more important is that, of which there is less. After the Bianco Grat, it is time to smell the hay in a valley like Val Fex; upon scaling Phrul-ri, to dwell in a warm, lush Nepali valley and drink water with a monk. And if you begin to itch again for reaching heights, for action, then you’d do well remembering that it is down here, in the valley, where it all begins; and ends.
